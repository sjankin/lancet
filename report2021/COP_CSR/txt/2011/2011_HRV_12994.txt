Biodiversity Close to, and partly within the Lonjsko polje nature park, which is the largest protected swamp land in the Danube basin and which is among the most threatened habitats in the world, are the INA’s plants which include gas fields, collecting oil and pipeline, oil wells and refinery Sisak refinery, which is only several kilometers away from the Lonjsko polje. In Nature Park Lonjsko polje, according to estimates of ornithologists, live 243 different bird species, some of which live exclusively in that area. There are about 600 breeding pairs of white storks and more than 50 pairs of black storks, more than 20 pairs of lesser spotted eagles and a hundred pairs of white spoonbills, and about 25 species, in European terms, endangered birds. Protected or restored habitats Two kilometers inside Lonjsko Polje nature park, Vrbak gas field is located in which the Oil and Gas E&P BD is continuously monitoring, with the help of activists from the Nature Park, the impact of gas production (noise, traffic, the presence of humans and other activities) on the birds habitat, especially corncrake bird which is an endangered and legally protected species. According to estimates, on the monitored area there are about 200 pairs of birds, and the technology did not affect their population growth. On facilities of Stružec plant white stork settled, which builds one of the largest nests in the bird world. Due to the large size and weight of the nest (on average 180x180x80 cm, but can reach up to 3 m, while the nest can weigh up to one tonne), E & P employees, in cooperation with the experts from the Lonjsko polje nature park, built platforms with carriers for nests . It is the first such report on the activities of the group as a whole, complementing the audited financial reports of INA Group and its major shareholder MOL. INA has a long history of reporting on its sustainability performance in Croatia, issuing annual sustainability reports since 2007, based on the GRI G3 guidelines, recognized as the leading global reporting framework. The report also cross references the reporting framework of the IPIECA (global oil and gas industry association for environmental and social issues) Guidance on voluntary sustainability reporting. It is noteworthy that INA has developed a profile as one of the leading and most consistent reporters on its sustainability performance in Croatia. INA is a member of the Global Compact initiative in Croatia since 2007, and has recently been recognized by the Principles for Responsible Investment for its high-quality sustainability report in 2009. This report continues in the same tradition, containing timely, balanced, and transparent reporting in line with the GRI G3 reporting framework. The inclusion of performance data for the group as a whole, whilst welcome, does make comparison over time somewhat difficult but comparative data is presented regarding most key indicators for at least two successive years (2009-2010) and, in many cases, for a longer period of time. There are some occasions when only data for 2010 is presented, notably in terms of the structure of suppliers, and fuel and energy consumption. Given the importance of these issues, it is to be hoped that comparative data will be made available in the future. The report contains a great deal of useful information regarding the profile of the company, its governance, mission, and goals, and in relation to its economic, environmental and social impacts. It will be of interest to a wide range of stakeholders including current and potential investors, business partners and suppliers, employees, specialist media, policy makers and specialists and advocates concerned with the oil and gas industry. At times, the report is hard to read, particularly for a non-specialist, mainly as a result of the use of technical concepts and acronyms. In future, I would recommend that a list of acronyms and a glossary of key technical terms be included to improve the clarity of the report for the lay reader. INA Group has an excellent track record of timely and consistent reporting to a high technical level so that it may soon be time to pause and reflect, through a survey and/or focus groups, on how the reports could be structured in future to meet the requirements of different and diverse stakeholders.
